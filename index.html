Advnture Time

Adventure time in the real world can include a wide range of activities such as biking, surfing, and paragliding. These activities are often referred to as adventure sports or extreme sports and are designed to provide an exciting or unusual experience with an uncertain outcome1. Adventure sports gained popularity in the 1960s and have since become a popular way for people to challenge themselves and experience the thrill of pushing their limits1.

Mountain biking is a sport of riding bicycles off-road, often over rough terrain, usually using specially designed mountain bikes1. This sport requires endurance, core and back strength, balance, bike handling skills, and self-reliance1. Mountain bikers ride on off-road trails such as singletrack, back-country roads, wider bike park trails, fire roads, and some advanced trails are designed with jumps, berms, and drop-offs to add excitement to the trail1. Mountain biking can generally be broken down into five distinct categories: cross country, trail riding, all mountain (also referred to as “Enduro”), downhill, and freeride

SURFING

Surfing is another popular adventure sport that involves riding waves on a surfboard. Surfers use their skills and knowledge of the ocean to catch waves and ride them towards the shore. Surfing can be done on various types of waves, from small beach breaks to large swells that produce massive waves

Surfing is a water sport that involves riding breaking waves towards the shore, usually on a surfboard1. The sport has its roots in premodern Hawaii and Polynesia, where it was practiced by both men and women from all social strata, from royalty to commoners1. In the early 20th century, surfing underwent a revival and quickly spread to California and Australia1. Today, surfing is enjoyed on beaches with breakers throughout the world, and several international championships are held2. The goal of surfing is to maneuver on the unbroken face of the wave, preferably as far back towards the curl (“tube”) as possible2. In addition to surfboards, surfers can use belly-boards, kneeboards, or kayaks, or they can bodysurf using no board at all

PARAGLIDING

Paragliding is an adventure sport that involves flying a paraglider, which is a lightweight, free-flying glider aircraft. Paragliders are launched by foot from a hill or mountain and can stay airborne for extended periods of time by using rising air currents. Paragliding offers a unique perspective of the world and allows pilots to soar like birds

Paragliding is a recreational and competitive adventure sport of flying paragliders, which are lightweight, free-flying, foot-launched glider aircraft with no rigid primary structure1. The pilot sits in a harness or in a cocoon-like ‘pod’ suspended below a fabric wing. The wing shape is maintained by the suspension lines, the pressure of air entering vents in the front of the wing, and the aerodynamic forces of the air flowing over the outside1. Paragliding can be traced back to the activities of French parachute inventor Pierre Lemoigne, who tow-launched his advanced round-parachute canopies in the 1950s. The ram-air-inflated cell structure originated in the early 1960s with the kite and parachute designs of Canadian-born American inventor Domina Jalbert2. Paragliders may be launched from flatland by towing, either with a winch or behind a vehicle. Takeoff and landing are on foot and usually occur on a hill or mountain
